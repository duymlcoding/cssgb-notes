## Customer Identification and Segmentation

Six Sigma projects begin by identifying internal and external customers whenever applicable. Customer segmentation is driven by customer requirements and often includes these categories:

- Internal and external
- Age groups, especially for consumer products
- Geographical location, including climate, language, and ethnicity
- Industry type, such as construction or agricultural

Within each segment, construct a listing of customers. When a project team proposes changes of any type, both internal and external customers must be consulted, or their concerns must be represented.

## Collecting Customer Feedback

Collect customer feedback through surveys, focus groups, interviews, and various forms of observation. The core principle is simple: ask customers what they need or want. Many managers and engineers assume they know customer desires because they are technical experts, but this leads to problems. A typical example is software programmers who release programs or patches without full testing, inevitably requiring new patches and rework.

To build support as the Green Belt on a project, first obtain benchmark process data. Then talk directly with the people who perform the work to understand what they do and what could make their jobs easier. Next, speak with individuals both upstream and downstream of the target process area. Their insights often reveal improvements that help the project succeed.

Effective data collection requires reviewing questions to eliminate vagueness, ambiguity, and any unintended bias. Question design should be clear, direct, and free from phrasing that leads respondents toward a particular answer.

## Voice of the Customer and Feedback Campaigns

Voice of the Customer (VOC) is a foundational quality concept. The goal of making a better, more consistent product is only confirmed when customers are consistently satisfied. VOC data must be collected before, during, and after improvement projects. Successful VOC programs are proactive, constant, and leverage technology to gather feedback through numerous channels.

Methods for capturing VOC feedback include:

- Surveys via telephone, mail, email, or online
- Focus groups in person or online
- Interviews
- Beta or user testing
- Feedback forms
- Customer complaints
- Social media or site interaction
- Reviews
- Forums

VOC campaigns serve three purposes: clarifying customer needs and desires, clarifying specific process problems, and acting as a regular part of improvement, customer service, and marketing.

### General Feedback Campaigns

General customer feedback is often obtained through feedback forms, complaint records, and passive website or social media monitoring. These methods test the overall customer mood and provide a high-level satisfaction reading, without necessarily pinpointing root causes.

A Kroger grocery store receipt survey illustrates a strong general VOC design. It asks for the date, time, entry code, and areas shopped. The survey then rates satisfaction on store attributes: overall satisfaction, employee friendliness, prices, service, cleanliness, item availability, weekly specials, ease of movement, quality of brands, and checkout times. A final question asks whether the shopper would recommend the store in the next 30 days. These questions are designed to gauge general feelings on critical-to-quality (CTQ) elements.

General VOC data functions as a smoke alarm. A smoke alarm is a sensitive indicator that alerts people to a potential fire, enabling early action. Similarly, a sudden drop in a satisfaction score, such as cleanliness at a specific store, triggers deeper investigation. Early action can prevent problems from escalating and becoming more costly. Some organizations also use general VOC scores as quality indicators for employees, tying rewards to satisfaction ratings to boost both performance and motivation.

### Specific Feedback Campaigns

Organizations often seek feedback specific to a problem, product, or idea. The same tools used for general campaigns can be applied, but the tool can be tailored to the need: a beta test for a new app, or a focus group to test a product or marketing campaign.

Specific feedback requires specific, targeted questions. This is critical when clarifying general feedback signals. If cleanliness scores drop, asking where and how customers observe uncleanliness prevents misguided action. Without that detail, managers might increase floor mopping when the real customer complaint is dim lighting or sloppily stocked shelves.

## Selecting the Right VOC Tools

Selecting a VOC tool balances cost, timeline, and the type of feedback needed. The table below summarizes each tool’s relative cost, benefits, and disadvantages.

- Feedback form
  - Cost: Low
  - Benefits: Gathers large volumes of data from many sources; can be structured to produce numeric data for easier analysis
  - Disadvantages: Only people who choose to respond leave feedback; skewed toward customers with strong feelings

- Telephone survey
  - Cost: High
  - Benefits: Random selection allows conclusions to be drawn for the entire population
  - Disadvantages: Requires substantial labor hours; customers may be annoyed by unwanted calls

- Mail survey
  - Cost: Medium
  - Benefits: Random selection enables population-level conclusions; lower cost alternative to phone or in-person surveys
  - Disadvantages: The customer must return the survey; low response rates force you to send many surveys to achieve a statistical sample

- Social media
  - Cost: Low
  - Benefits: Ongoing ability to seek feedback; ability to ask questions on the fly; possibly the least expensive VOC option
  - Disadvantages: Requires an established social media following; relies on followers and fans, meaning you ask feedback from people who already favor your brand

- In-person focus groups
  - Cost: High
  - Benefits: Moderators can seek in-depth answers or immediate follow-up
  - Disadvantages: Limits data pool to local customers or those willing to travel; cannot generalize to the general population; customers may be less honest face-to-face

- Online focus groups
  - Cost: Low
  - Benefits: Moderators can obtain in-depth answers immediately; no travel requirement
  - Disadvantages: Cannot generalize findings to the general population

## Applying Pareto Charts to Customer Feedback

Pareto charts are helpful analytical tools when analyzing frequencies or causes of problems uncovered by VOC data. They allow teams to narrow a broad set of issues and focus on a single cause that yields large results across the entire process. Because Pareto charts present complex data as an ordered bar chart, they are immediately understandable to business leaders and others outside Six Sigma. Including a Pareto chart when presenting surprising or critical VOC findings provides visual reinforcement and speeds decision-making.

## Exam Tips

- When given a data collection instrument, identify questions that contain vague wording or leading language, and rewrite them to eliminate ambiguity and unintended bias.
- Given a scenario, distinguish between a general VOC campaign (broad satisfaction monitoring) and a specific feedback campaign (targeted investigation of a known problem) and select the appropriate questioning approach.
- Choose the correct VOC tool for a scenario by evaluating cost, ability to generalize to the population, and the risk of response bias. For example, mail surveys allow random sampling but require large mailings due to low response rates; social media is low-cost but biased toward existing brand followers.
- Interpret a sudden drop in a satisfaction metric as a smoke alarm signal, and propose a specific feedback campaign to uncover the root cause (asking where, when, and how the issue is observed) before taking corrective action.
- Use a Pareto chart to prioritize the most frequent customer complaint categories so the team can address the vital few causes first.
