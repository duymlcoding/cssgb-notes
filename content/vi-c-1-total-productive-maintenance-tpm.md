## TPM in the Lean Six Sigma Framework

Total Productive Maintenance (TPM) appears in the ASQ CSSGB Handbook (Chapter 23) as a Lean tool that supports process control. In the DMAIC matrix, TPM is placed under the Measure phase and associated with the Lean phase “Plan improvement.” The Handbook groups TPM with other Lean concepts such as the seven wastes, overall equipment effectiveness (OEE), kanban, and the visual factory. While the printed chapter provides an introduction, the complete procedural details for TPM are delivered through CD-ROM video files. This positioning within the Measure phase and the Plan improvement step signals that TPM is an enabler for stable processes: it builds the foundation of reliable equipment so that data collection, capability studies, and control charts produce meaningful results. Without dependable machinery, variation from equipment breakdowns masks the true process capability.

## Visual Management that Supports TPM

Although the Handbook does not provide a step-by-step TPM procedure in the supplied pages, it describes several visual-workplace practices that directly serve a TPM environment. These practices make equipment status immediately obvious, reduce downtime, and prevent defects caused by improperly maintained assets.

- **Equipment status indicators.** Maintenance personnel use visual controls to show whether a machine is “up,” “down,” or under preventive maintenance. Stack lights (red, yellow, green) fitted to machines operate on the same principle: green means normal operation, yellow signals an operator needs help, and red means the process has stopped. This visual andon capability allows rapid response and prevents a small interruption from escalating.
- **Lock out tag out (LOTO).** In high-risk areas, LOTO devices provide a visual and physical barrier that prevents the inadvertent start-up of equipment under service. The clear visual signal protects operator safety and ensures that maintenance work happens without introducing new hazards.
- **Analog gauge limits.** A simple cut-paper sector placed on an analog gauge marks the safe operating range. This visual aid helps operators stay within that range and quickly spot when equipment requires servicing. Digital gauges achieve the same effect with audio alarms and indicator lights.
- **Hour-by-hour output charts.** Where flow has been established and output is paced to takt time, teams use visual charts that compare expected production per pitch (a set time interval) with actual output. Color coding (green for met, red for not met) highlights equipment-related shortfalls in real time and prompts immediate root-cause investigation.
- **Standard work with visual defect examples.** Training inspectors with visual defect boards reduces the chance of accepting nonconforming product, which could otherwise hide an equipment problem. This visual standard ensures that operators and inspectors maintain consistent quality while guarding against equipment-driven variation.
- **Color-coded file and inventory systems.** While these primarily reduce search time, they also prevent mix-ups that could cause equipment to run with wrong parts or materials. The visual signal supports the TPM goal of eliminating waste caused by searching, waiting, and correcting errors.

These visual techniques turn maintenance and production data into signals that everyone can understand. The Handbook emphasizes that a visual system succeeds only when employees and management respond to the signals. If an alarm light is ignored, if depleted stock is not replenished, or if a visual defect passes unnoticed, then no visual workplace, and by extension no TPM program, can deliver its intended benefit.

## Relationship to Other Lean Concepts

The Handbook lists TPM alongside the seven wastes and overall equipment effectiveness. This suggests that TPM directly attacks the waste of waiting, defects, and overprocessing that stem from unreliable equipment. OEE is a key metric that quantifies availability, performance, and quality, all areas TPM seeks to improve. Kanban, also listed, becomes more reliable when machines run predictably. The visual factory provides the communication infrastructure that TPM relies on. Together, these concepts create a self-reinforcing system: stable equipment enables true pull production, while visual signals keep everyone aligned on current conditions.

## Role in the Control Phase

During Control, a Six Sigma team hands the improved process back to the process owner. TPM practices ensure that the equipment state that enabled the improvement is sustained. The team might incorporate TPM elements into the control plan, specifying who monitors equipment status, what measures indicate a developing problem, and how to respond. Visual tools such as stack lights, limit markers, and hour-by-hour charts become day-to-day controls that prevent backsliding. The process owner does not need advanced statistical training to read these visual cues. This aligns with the Handbook’s guidance on the visual workplace: the goal is to make the comparison of actual performance with expected performance easy and accessible by all employees.

## Exam Tips

- Recognize that TPM is classified as a Lean tool and, in the DMAIC structure, is placed under the Measure phase with a Lean focus of “Plan improvement.”
- Associate TPM with the overall equipment effectiveness (OEE) metric and the drive to eliminate the seven wastes, as they are presented together in the Handbook’s Lean concepts summary.
- Understand that visual workplace techniques (stack lights, gauge limit markers, hour-by-hour charts, LOTO) are critical enablers of TPM; the exam may test your ability to identify which visual control supports equipment stability.
- Remember that the success of TPM depends on active response to visual signals; a control system that only displays information but does not prompt corrective action will fail.
- Link TPM to the Control phase by noting that well-maintained equipment sustains the process improvements handed over to the process owner, often through visual controls that require minimal statistical expertise.
