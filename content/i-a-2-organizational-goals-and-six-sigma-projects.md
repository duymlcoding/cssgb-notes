## Voice of the Customer (VOC)

The Voice of the Customer is the process for capturing customer-related information. This proactive, continuously innovative process seeks to gather stated, unstated, and anticipated customer requirements, needs, and desires. The purpose is to achieve customer loyalty and build lasting relationships. VOC data influences project selection by revealing which improvements will most strengthen customer acquisition, satisfaction, loyalty, retention, and business sustainability.

The VOC approach integrates many data sources. Among them are survey data, focus group findings, web-based data, warranty data, complaint logs, field reports, and any other information that affects purchasing and relationship decisions. When a Six Sigma team understands the VOC, it can align project goals with what customers truly value, ensuring that improvement efforts directly support the organization’s market position.

## Balanced Scorecard and Key Performance Indicators

Organizations use a balanced scorecard to translate vision and strategy into measurable actions. The balanced scorecard is both a measurement system and a management system. It examines four perspectives:

- Financial
- Customer
- Internal processes
- Employee learning and growth

Within each perspective, the scorecard tracks financial and nonfinancial metrics, as well as lagging and leading measures. Lagging measures are taken at the end of an event; leading measures are tracked upstream and help achieve objectives. This balance prevents a narrow focus on short-term financial results and keeps improvement projects aligned with long-term strategy.

A Key Process Indicator (KPI) is a quantifiable measurement, agreed upon beforehand, that reflects the critical success factors of a department or group. KPIs differ from company to company and from process to process. Because many potential measures exist, the challenge lies in sifting through collected data to identify the few measures that truly drive the desired outcome. Management teams typically establish the KPIs that projects must track, linking every improvement effort to the organization’s high-level goals.

## Dashboards for Real-Time Monitoring

A dashboard (or scoreboard) is a visual representation that allows personnel to view company performance in real time. It serves as a critical decision-making tool in day-to-day operations, helping employees predict sales, cash flow, and profit. By presenting KPIs and other metrics graphically, a dashboard empowers team members and business owners to make decisions that drive cash flow and profitability. When dashboards are built with knowledge of industry averages, historical performance, and a balanced view of the entire company, they reinforce the alignment between daily work and strategic objectives.

## Project Viability Matrix

Not every improvement idea fits the DMAIC structure. To determine whether a proposed project is viable and aligned with organizational goals, teams can apply a weighted criteria matrix. The matrix includes 15 questions, each addressing a critical factor. Examples of these factors include whether a sponsor or champion exists, whether project goals align with corporate goals, whether customer and company benefits are present, and whether data, team members, and resources are available.

Each question is assigned a weight from 1 (least important) to 5 (most important). The questions are answered with one of five responses: No (1), Mostly No (2), Possibly (3), Mostly Yes (4), or Yes (5). The completed grid produces a project score interpreted against a viability scale.

**Step-by-step calculation of the viability score**

The calculation proceeds as follows:

1. Divide each weight by 3 to obtain a multiplier. A weight of 3 gives 1; a weight of 5 gives 5/3 (approximately 1.7).
2. Multiply the numeric response value (1 through 5, corresponding to the column) by the multiplier from Step 1, and place the result in the appropriate column.
3. Sum all the weighted values within each of the five response columns.
4. Multiply each column sum by the column number (1 for No, 2 for Mostly No, 3 for Possibly, 4 for Mostly Yes, 5 for Yes).
5. Add the five products from Step 4 to obtain a total score numerator.
6. Sum the five column totals from Step 3 to obtain the denominator.
7. Divide the numerator from Step 5 by the denominator from Step 6. The result is the project viability score.

**Numerical example**

A team evaluating a carpet installation process applied the matrix. After weighting each criterion and marking the appropriate response column, the weighted column sums were:

- No column sum: 1.3
- Mostly No column sum: 4.4
- Possibly column sum: 4.7
- Mostly Yes column sum: 3.3
- Yes column sum: 4.4

The calculations continue:

- 1.3 × 1 = 1.3
- 4.4 × 2 = 8.8
- 4.7 × 3 = 14.1
- 3.3 × 4 = 13.2
- 4.4 × 5 = 22.0

Sum of the products: 1.3 + 8.8 + 14.1 + 13.2 + 22.0 = 59.4

Sum of the column totals (denominator): 1.3 + 4.4 + 4.7 + 3.3 + 4.4 = 18.1

Final score: 59.4 ÷ 18.1 = 3.28

**Interpreting the score**

- Score below 2.0: Not viable for DMAIC
- Score between 2.0 and 3.0: Possibly viable, but further validation is needed
- Score above 3.0: A viable DMAIC project

In the example, the score of 3.28 confirms the project is viable for DMAIC. This structured approach ensures that selected projects have strong ties to customer benefits, corporate objectives, data availability, and resource feasibility, all of which are essential for achieving organizational goals through Six Sigma.

## Exam Tips

- Expect questions that ask you to identify the four balanced scorecard perspectives: financial, customer, internal processes, and employee learning and growth.
- Be able to distinguish between lagging measures (end of event) and leading measures (upstream, predictive). Understand that the balanced scorecard deliberately includes both.
- Know that a KPI is a pre-agreed quantifiable measurement that reflects the critical success factors of a specific department or process, and that identifying the right KPIs from abundant data is a key challenge.
- You may be presented with a project viability matrix and asked to compute the score. Memorize the steps: divide weight by 3, multiply by response column number, sum columns, multiply column sums by column numbers, total those products, divide by sum of column sums, and compare to thresholds.
- Recognize that the VOC process captures not only stated requirements but also unstated and anticipated needs, and that it draws from diverse sources such as surveys, complaint logs, and warranty data.
