## Negative Team Dynamics and Countermeasures

Six Sigma teams encounter predictable dysfunctions that delay progress and degrade results. Green Belts must recognize each dynamic by its symptoms, understand its typical causes, and apply the appropriate countermeasure.

- **Unquestioned acceptance of opinions as facts**  
  Symptoms: members present information without supporting data or analysis, offer unfounded assumptions, and treat opinions as evidence. Probable causes include an organizational culture that does not insist on facts and a lack of management by fact. Countermeasures: the team leader requests supporting data, analysis, and conclusions that are statistically valid; assumptions behind any analysis are challenged openly.

- **Groupthink**  
  Symptoms: no public disagreements occur, doubts are voiced only in private, and several classic warning signs appear (suppression of dissent, illusion of unanimity). The probable cause is a fear that group cohesiveness will be lost if disagreement surfaces, making harmony the paramount goal. Countermeasures: bring in independent members from outside the group, rotate the roles and responsibilities of members at milestones, and enforce management by fact.

- **Feuding**  
  Symptoms: hostilities produce heated arguments, slow progress, and low team morale. Causes are poor conflict resolution, lack of mutual respect, and failure to enforce operating ground rules. Countermeasures: confront the adversaries offline, never in the team meeting (public confrontation worsens the situation); enforce discipline and emphasize mutual respect among team members; restate the objective of the team as the main focus.

- **Rush to accomplishment**  
  Symptoms: incomplete data collection, inconsistent analysis, and an urgency to reach a conclusion quickly. Probable causes include an unrealistic deadline, untrained team members, and a short-term gain mindset. Countermeasures: the team leadership insists on thorough data collection, analysis, and statistical significance; asks for alternate solutions; revises the deadline to a realistic schedule based on available resources.

- **Attribution (casual references, emotional judgments)**  
  Symptoms: members make casual references, do not seek explanations, and prefer psychological or emotional judgments. Causes overlap with those of the rush to accomplishment. Countermeasures: challenge the assumptions made by team members; use a devil’s advocate approach; ask for the analysis behind any conclusion drawn.

- **Discounts**  
  Symptoms: members’ opinions are ignored, sarcasm surfaces, team morale is low, and people do not listen to one another. Countermeasures: encourage mutual respect, enforce discipline, and ask the member providing an opinion for full clarification.

- **Digressions and tangents**  
  Symptoms: discussion strays outside the meeting scope or agenda, distractions dominate, and meeting time yields little progress. Probable causes include major organizational change, cultural issues, and a lack of focused leadership. Countermeasures: enforce compliance to the agenda items and time allotment; restate the meeting ground rules; redirect discussions back to the topic.

## Six Sigma Organizational Structures

Six Sigma demands a clear organizational structure to ensure accountability for the investment in training, resources, and consistent methodology. Smaller organizations may combine roles, but the responsibilities must still be fulfilled.

A typical **large Six Sigma organization** places the executive sponsor(s) at the top (VP quality or business unit leader), followed by multiple champions, one or more Master Black Belts, Black Belts, Green Belts, and project team members. Process owners sit alongside the project teams and interact with both champions and Black Belts.  

A typical **small Six Sigma organization** replaces the layered belt hierarchy with a quality engineer, technical support personnel, and the quality department or operational excellence group. Each project is led by a project leader who oversees a team, while the overall structure remains lean.

## Core Team Roles and Responsibilities

### Executive Sponsor
- **Candidate**: business unit leader responsible for profit and loss, usually at director level or above.  
- **Training/background**: Six Sigma concepts, strategies, overview, and operational definitions.  
- **Primary responsibilities**  
  - Set direction and priorities for the Six Sigma organization.  
  - Allocate resources for projects.  
  - Set the Six Sigma vision and overall program objectives.  
  - Monitor the progress of the overall program.  
  - Initiate incentive programs and reward successful projects.

### Champion
- **Candidate**: typically upper-level managers.  
- **Training/background**: Six Sigma concepts, strategies, tools, methods, and operational definitions, with emphasis on management tools.  
- **Primary responsibilities**  
  - Liaise with senior management.  
  - Allocate resources for projects.  
  - Determine project selection criteria.  
  - Remove barriers that hinder project success.  
  - Approve completed projects and implement change.  
  - Coach the team at the project charter stage and provide input on scope and team composition.  
  - Locate resources (money, equipment, time, labor) and handle political obstacles so the team can focus on the work.  
  - Work with other managers to transition improvements into daily operations.

### Process Owner
- **Candidate**: an individual responsible and accountable for the execution and results of the process; a sponsor or champion may also serve as process owner.  
- **Training/background**: Six Sigma concepts, strategies, tools, methods, and operational definitions, with emphasis on statistical tools.  
- **Primary responsibilities**  
  - Select team members.  
  - Allocate resources for projects.  
  - Provide deep process knowledge and often act as a process expert.  
  - Review process changes, approve changes, and support change management.  
  - Implement change and ensure that improvements are sustained.  
  - Receive the solution after the project transitions, maintain and monitor the controls created by the team.

Six Sigma leaders must watch for process owners who resist change or believe they have all the answers. A process owner who fears being outshone may block team participation, requiring tactful intervention from the Black Belt and champion.

### Master Black Belt (MBB)
- **Candidate**: individuals trained in Six Sigma methodologies, statistical tools, basic financial tools, change management, risk assessment, project management, executive communication, and highly experienced in teaching, coaching, and mentoring Black Belts and Green Belts. This is always a full-time position.  
- **Training/background**: Six Sigma Body of Knowledge, lean enterprise synergy, finance for nonfinancial managers, risk assessment, project management, change agent skills, MBB train-the-trainer skills, presentation, communication, leadership, and facilitation.  
- **Primary responsibilities**  
  - Coach Six Sigma Black Belts and Green Belts.  
  - Utilize resources provided by management effectively.  
  - Formulate overall business strategy linked to the Six Sigma program.  
  - Monitor project progress closely; typically oversee 15–20 projects at a time.  
  - Provide coaching and mentoring for new Black Belts and Green Belts.  
  - Work with champions and process owners to select projects.  
  - Address issues of project stagnation.  
  - Remove barriers hindering project success.  
  - Act as a subject matter expert for the organization.  
  - Review and approve completed projects.  
  - Share lessons learned with the extended team.  
  - Provide input to the rewards committee.

### Black Belt
- **Candidate**: individuals trained in Six Sigma methodologies, statistical tools, basic financial tools, change management, risk assessment, and project management, with experience managing Black Belt projects. Always a full-time position.  
- **Training/background**: Six Sigma Black Belt Body of Knowledge, lean enterprise synergy, finance for nonfinancial managers, risk assessment, project management, change agent skills, presentation, communication, leadership and facilitation skills. Certified as a Six Sigma Black Belt.  
- **Primary responsibilities**  
  - Lead and manage Six Sigma projects.  
  - Utilize resources provided by management effectively.  
  - Provide net present value (NPV), return on investment (ROI), and payback calculations for projects.  
  - Work full-time on four to six projects per year.  
  - Monitor project progress closely.  
  - Follow the DMAIC process and apply appropriate statistical methods.  
  - Work with champions, Master Black Belts, and process owners for project selection.  
  - Address issues of project stagnation, consulting the Master Black Belt when needed.  
  - Remove barriers hindering success.  
  - Update and present project progress to management.  
  - Review completed projects and share lessons learned.  
  - Create a rationale for the project, provide input on team selection, educate team members on tools, maintain schedules, provide statistical expertise, assist with transition, and deliver end-of-project documentation.

### Green Belt
- **Candidate**: individuals trained in Six Sigma methodologies, basic statistical tools, and process improvement techniques. This is typically a full-time position, though some organizations make it part of an existing job.  
- **Training/background**: Six Sigma Green Belt Body of Knowledge, lean enterprise synergy, presentation skills, communication skills. Certified as a Six Sigma Green Belt.  
- **Primary responsibilities**  
  - Support Six Sigma projects that have higher ROI.  
  - Lead smaller projects with moderate savings and ROI.  
  - Follow the DMAIC process and apply appropriate statistical methods.  
  - Review the approach periodically with an experienced Black Belt and Master Black Belt.  
  - Provide input to the Master Black Belt, Black Belt, and process owners during project selection.  
  - Identify issues of project stagnation; consult the Black Belt or Master Black Belt.  
  - Identify and report barriers hindering success.  
  - Share lessons learned with the extended team.

### Project Team Member
- **Candidate**: selected by the process owner and trained in Six Sigma methodologies, quality tools, basic statistical tools, and process improvement techniques.  
- **Training/background**: Six Sigma methodologies, quality tools, process improvement, and teamwork.  
- **Primary responsibilities**  
  - Support and contribute to Six Sigma projects.  
  - Participate in charter and scope definition.  
  - Provide inputs during project meetings and brainstorm ideas.  
  - Help collect data where responsible.  
  - Follow the DMAIC process and apply appropriate tools.  
  - Review the approach periodically with the Green Belt and experienced Black Belt.  
  - Provide input to the Green Belt, Black Belt, and process owners during the project.

## Additional Supporting Roles

### Project Manager
Some organizations pair traditional project management with Six Sigma. The project manager does not lead the team but supports the Black Belt by managing documentation, timelines, meeting follow-ups, and keeping meetings on track. This allows the Black Belt to focus on brainstorming, statistical analysis, and team facilitation.

### Timekeeper
A timekeeper helps meetings stay on schedule, reduces scope creep, and increases productivity. The timekeeper uses a detailed agenda provided by the Black Belt or project manager. Responsibilities include warning the team when time for an agenda item is almost up and signalling when time has elapsed. The timekeeper must be organized and level-headed, and the project leader must back the timekeeper’s ability to interrupt politely. Leaders can still choose to extend a discussion, but the timekeeper’s signals ensure deliberate choices.

### Scribe (Minute-Taker)
One person records the discussions, decisions, diagrams, and brainstorming output of each meeting. The Black Belt should never act as scribe. The scribe types and disseminates meeting minutes, including photographs of whiteboard diagrams or sticky-note arrangements, so that the team has an accurate, shareable record. Team members can review the notes, and organizations often store them in a shared portal.

## Types of Team Members and Selection Principles

### Three Participation Levels
- **Regular team members** attend all or nearly all meetings and participate in all team activities. They include the project leader, process owner, and identified subject matter experts critical to the group.
- **Ad hoc team members** provide expertise on an as-needed basis, typically employees who work directly with the process. They are called into meetings only when additional information or assistance is required, so that day-to-day production is not harmed.
- **Resource team members** are included solely when the project leader believes their expert information, counsel, or resource access is needed. They often come from ancillary departments such as accounting, human resources, compliance, or related functional areas.

### Selection Tips
The ideal regular team size is about five members. Larger teams create communication problems, complicate brainstorming, and increase burnout risk. Regular members should not serve on more than one team while handling a full daily workload; leaders may reduce normal work requirements for full-time team members.

When choosing members, organizations should:
- Select employees knowledgeable about the customer, product, or process.
- Choose those with a demonstrated willingness and ability to work in teams.
- Prefer employees who have access to and understand the data needed to measure the process.
- Ensure each member can commit at least five hours per week.
- Match skills to the project’s nature (for example, avoid adding a marketing expert to a purely technical improvement).
- Consider including a known political obstacle on the team, as this can increase that person’s buy-in.

## Exam Tips

1. Given a scenario where a team leader notices members never openly disagree but confide doubts privately afterward, identify the dynamic as groupthink and select the countermeasure of bringing in outside independent members or rotating roles.
2. A champion is asked to resolve a conflict between two feuding team members. Based on the countermeasures, recommend confronting the adversaries offline rather than in the team meeting, and restating the team’s objective to refocus everyone.
3. When asked which role is responsible for ensuring that process improvements are sustained after a project closes, identify the process owner, who receives the solution and maintains controls.
4. A Black Belt is overseeing 18 projects and coaching several Green Belts while also setting organizational strategy. Match this scope of responsibility to the Master Black Belt role.
5. A Green Belt leading a small project with moderate savings encounters stagnation. According to role responsibilities, the Green Belt should consult a Black Belt or Master Black Belt, not resolve the issue alone.
6. During team formation, a sponsor asks the Green Belt for the recommended number of regular team members. Recall that about five members is ideal, and explain that larger teams can cause communication problems and burnout.
