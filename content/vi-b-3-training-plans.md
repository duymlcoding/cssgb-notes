## Training Plans

Training plans in the Control phase ensure that process owners and business teams can sustain improvements after the Six Sigma project closes. Much of the Control phase activity concentrates on transitioning process ownership away from the improvement team, and that transition often requires deliberate education. The source material from the CSSC Training Manual notes that process owners and their teams may not be familiar with the statistical controls used during DMAIC. Therefore, appropriate documentation and education help them maintain the process and recognize when it is out of control.

The training plan is not a standalone document but is embedded in the control plan and supporting visual management tools. The control plan serves as a concise reference that instructs the business team on what to monitor, how to monitor, acceptable ranges, and corrective actions. This documentation functions as just-in-time training, especially when it includes clear measurement methods, standardized equipment, and step-by-step reaction procedures. The example control plan for the chocolate bar process shows how a simple table can specify the employee responsible, the unit of measurement, the method of measurement, the sample size, the frequency, and the location for recording data. For corrective action, the plan describes exact steps and references any associated standard operating procedures by number. By reading and using the control plan, operators learn exactly what to do without needing deep SPC expertise.

### Transitioning Knowledge to the Process Owner

The Six Sigma team works to hand the process back to its owner during Control. The CSSC manual states that the team must provide the process owner and business team with all tools and information required to maintain improvements. This duty appears explicitly on the Control Tollgate Checklist. Providing those tools often requires training sessions in which team members demonstrate how to collect data, interpret control charts, and apply corrective actions. The goal is to make the process self-sufficient, even if the operators do not have advanced statistical training.

The control plan itself is a training instrument. Its common fields include the process step, the critical-to-quality metric, specification limits, measurement method, sample size and frequency, responsible person, data recording location, and corrective actions. When a process owner trains a new employee, the control plan becomes the primary job aid because it distills complex monitoring requirements into a single, easy-to-reference page. Teams are encouraged to build corrective actions that the operator can carry out directly, as seen in the sugar disbursement example where the mixer operator calibrates the machine following a standard operating procedure. This design reduces downtime and gives operators greater ownership, which is itself a form of continuous learning.

### Visual Management and Standard Operating Procedure Posters

Visual management tools support training by turning standard operating procedures into highly visible, picture-based instructions. The CSSC manual lists examples such as signs, posted matrixes, auditing boards, color coding, safety signals, and pictorial representations of equipment operation. These visual aids shorten the learning curve for employees and reduce errors. A coffee shop might post a visual chart of drink ingredients, allowing baristas to prepare beverages correctly without memorizing every combination. In a medical environment, a poster might depict hand-washing protocols and bed operation steps. Office copy machines often use illustrated instructions or animated LED displays to guide users through jam clearance. Six Sigma teams can create similar job aids as part of the training plan, making the improved process easy to adopt and maintain long after the project ends.

### Preparing Operators to Read Control Charts

When automated data collection is available, teams can display real-time control charts on dashboards. The CSSC manual explains that business teams do not need to be well-versed in statistical process control to learn the eight tests for instability. The eight tests serve as simple decision rules: a point outside the control limits, nine points on one side of the center line, six points trending in one direction, fourteen points alternating up and down, two out of three points in Zone A, four out of five points in Zone B or beyond, fifteen points within Zone C, and eight points in a row on either side with none in Zone C. The training plan can include a short session or a reference card explaining these tests so that process owners know when to investigate and when to leave the process alone. Automation removes the burden of manual charting, but the team still trains staff on reading the displays and taking prescribed corrective actions.

### Team Reflection and Capturing Lessons Learned

The Control phase closes with a team celebration and reflection meeting. During this meeting, the team discusses lessons learned and captures ideas for future improvement projects. This is a knowledge-capture activity that feeds into organizational learning. While the team members’ thoughts are fresh, they can record insights about what worked, what surprised them, and where additional gains might lie. The Black Belt or project leader can later share those ideas with Six Sigma leadership panels. The meeting thus acts as a final training loop, making sure that tacit knowledge gets documented before the team disbands.

## Exam Tips

- When a question asks about training plans in the Control phase, connect your answer directly to the control plan document: the control plan specifies who measures what, how often, and what corrective action to take, and these instructions serve as operator training.
- Know that the Control Tollgate Checklist requires the team to have provided the process owner and business team with all tools and information necessary to maintain improvements; this implies the existence of some form of training or knowledge transfer.
- Expect scenarios where an operator must respond to an out-of-control signal. Use the eight control chart tests as part of the training content; a correct answer will describe training operators to recognize these patterns instead of requiring them to calculate control limits.
- If an exam item describes a team creating visual aids such as posters, pictorial SOPs, or color-coded boards during Control, recognize this as part of a training plan designed to reduce errors and ease operator onboarding.
- In questions about handoff to the process owner, the correct choice will emphasize practical documentation and simple, built-in corrective actions that the operator can perform, not complex statistical re-training.
