## Value as the Primary Organizational Driver

Value is the single most important concept driving organizational decisions in Six Sigma.
The customer defines value through their perception of a product’s usefulness and necessity.
Japanese and German carmakers succeed in the same markets because different customer segments define value differently: some prioritize reliability and fuel efficiency, others prize pride of ownership or rugged utility.
Once an organization understands what the customer values, it can determine a target cost.
That target cost blends current competitor selling prices with aggressive elimination of waste through lean methods.

### Value-Added vs. Non-Value-Added Activities

Lean thinking categorizes every process step by its contribution to value.
A step is value-added only when it satisfies three conditions simultaneously:

- The customer recognizes the value.
- The step changes the form, fit, or function of the product or service.
- The step is performed correctly the first time.

Activities that do not meet these criteria are non-value-added.
Rework is the classic example: a customer is willing to pay for a printed document but will not pay for corrections caused by supplier errors.
Inspection often occupies a grey area.
Testing a furnace to satisfy a mandatory safety listing is value-added because the customer values the certification; inspecting an incapable process to sort out defects is not value-added, because the permanent solution is to improve the process capability until inspection becomes unnecessary.
Studies show that an overwhelming percentage of total lead time consists of non-value-added waiting, so eliminating these activities is often more powerful than accelerating value-added steps.

## Metrics and Problem Quantification

Organizations use metrics to connect operational issues to financial and customer impacts.
A well-crafted problem statement transforms a vague complaint into a measurable case for action.
The problem statement answers five specific questions:

- Where did the problem occur?
- When did the problem occur?
- What process did the problem involve?
- How is the problem measured? (the specific metric)
- How much is the problem costing in money, time, customer satisfaction, or another critical metric?

A strong problem statement allows a busy executive to grasp the issue immediately and permits the team to derive an objective directly from it.
Consider this example from a distribution center:

"In the first quarter, the California distribution center sent 108,000 packages. Of those packages, 15,000 were returned, resulting in a 13.8 percent return rate. The rate of return is above the accepted 7 percent rate and cost the company an additional \\$372,000 for the quarter. Over the course of the year, the current process could result in additional costs of over \$1.4 million."

The return rate metric is calculated as:

$$
\text{Return Rate} = \frac{15{,}000}{108{,}000} \times 100 = 13.8\%
$$

The quarterly excess cost is \\$372,000, so the annualized potential cost becomes:

$$
\text{Annualized Cost} = $372{,}000 \times 4 = $1{,}488{,}000 \quad (\text{over } $1.4 \text{ million})
$$

A weak statement might say only "The Canton, Ohio bakery is producing undercooked bread." That statement omits the time frame, the magnitude, the metric, and the financial impact.
Without quantified metrics, leadership cannot prioritize resources.

### Using 5 Whys to Link Metrics to Root Causes

When a problem is defined with a metric, the 5 Whys tool systematically connects the observed metric to its root cause.
The procedure is simple and works well when processes involve human interactions:

1. Gather subject-matter experts close to the process.
2. Write a basic problem statement (for example, "Customers are dissatisfied with hamburgers").
3. Ask the highest-level "Why?" question: "Why are customers dissatisfied with hamburgers?"
4. Record the answer: "The meat is undercooked."
5. Form the next "Why?" question from that answer: "Why is the meat undercooked?"
6. Continue until the team reaches an actionable root cause.

In the hamburger example, four whys revealed that a new grill cook was not trained on calibration, causing inconsistent grill performance and undercooked meat.
A fifth why could uncover a missing training policy, pointing toward a long-term control solution.
The tool costs little time and forces the team to ground every layer of cause in observable facts, which directly informs the metrics needing improvement.

## Lean Tools and Operational Stability

Foundational lean tools such as 5S create the stable environment needed for reliable metrics and sustained improvement.
5S proceeds through five steps:

- **Sort:** Remove unneeded items from the work area. Items that are never used are discarded immediately.
- **Set in order:** Arrange required items for easy accessibility. Designate marked locations so that everything has a place.
- **Shine:** Clean the work area and equipment. Effective cleaning often uncovers quality issues, improves measurement accuracy, and prevents accidents.

These steps address the reality that a cluttered workbench wastes skilled workers’ time and introduces errors.
5S is typically one of the first tools applied on the path to a lean enterprise, because it directly impacts process control, safety, and the trustworthiness of measurement data.

## Exam Tips

- Know the three criteria for a value-added step (customer recognizes value, transformation occurs, done right the first time) and be able to classify inspection scenarios as value-added or non-value-added based on whether the customer willingly pays for the activity.
- Be able to evaluate a problem statement for completeness using the five checklist questions (where, when, what process, metric, cost/magnitude); the exam may ask you to identify what is missing.
- Understand that 5 Whys is most effective when processes involve human inputs and that it is used to move from a general problem statement to a specific root cause, not to jump to solutions prematurely.
- Recognize that 5S is a foundational workplace organization method (Sort, Set in order, Shine are the first three steps) and that it supports measurement reliability and waste reduction, not just housekeeping.
