## Introduction

Effective team communication in the Define phase lays the foundation for project alignment, realistic expectations, and disciplined execution. Six Sigma teams, sponsors, and champions must stay coordinated on timing, resources, constraints, and what constitutes a successful outcome. The following topics cover the primary communication tools and practices that a Green Belt uses to keep all stakeholders informed and engaged.

## Phase-Based Timeline Estimation

A Black Belt or Six Sigma leader often begins project planning by constructing a raw, phase-based timeline. This approach assigns a number of weeks to each DMAIC phase based on the total allowable project duration, which may be derived from leadership deadlines or an overall time estimate. For example, a four-month (16-week) improvement effort might allocate three weeks to Define and five weeks to Measure. Because phases naturally overlap, the Measure phase can start before Define closes and continue into Analyze. The leader sketches a simple Gantt-style bar chart that shows the phases stacked in time.

This rapid estimation helps the team communicate an initial schedule to leadership and members. The main risk is that someone without Six Sigma experience or deep process knowledge may misjudge phase durations. When presenting the phase-based timeline, the leader must emphasize that the dates are rough estimates and that the duration of each phase can change as the project unfolds. This prevents leadership from treating the timeline as a hard commitment and avoids the frustration of unrealistic expectations.

## Critical Path Method for Detailed Scheduling

Once a project is underway and the team has gathered more information, a detailed schedule can be built using the critical path method. This technique requires more input from team members but produces a realistic, activity-level timeline for a project or a single phase. The steps to create a critical path diagram follow a clear sequence.

1. Identify the critical activities needed to complete the project or phase.
2. Put those activities in the order they must be performed.
3. Assign a time estimate (in days or weeks) to each activity.
4. Draw the diagram from left to right, stacking activities that can occur in parallel. Earlier activities sit to the left; later activities sit to the right.
5. Draw the critical path through the stacked activities. When two or more activities run simultaneously, the critical branch is the one with the longest duration.
6. Add the durations along the critical path to obtain the total estimated time.

### Worked Example

In the Define phase of a project to reduce uncollected invoices, the team identifies four critical activities:

| Activity | Order | Duration |
|----------|-------|----------|
| Choose a team | 1 | 1 week |
| Create a project charter | 2 (can be done in parallel with problem statement) | 1 week |
| Define the problem (problem statement) | 2 (parallel with charter) | 1 day |
| Create a baseline metric | 3 | 2 weeks |

The diagram places “Choose a team” first. On its right, the charter and the problem statement are stacked because they can proceed simultaneously. The critical path goes through the charter because 1 week is longer than 1 day. Finally, “Create a baseline metric” follows. Adding the longest durations gives a total Define-phase duration of 1 week (team) + 1 week (charter) + 2 weeks (baseline metric) = 4 weeks.

This method yields a more reliable schedule and helps the team communicate task dependencies and realistic completion dates to the sponsor.

## Milestone Meetings

Once a timeline exists, milestone meetings keep the team on track and inform the champion or sponsor of progress. In a DMAIC project, milestones are typically set at the end of each phase (Define, Measure, Analyze, Improve, Control). Each milestone date is when the team or Black Belt meets with the sponsor to present phase findings and results, creating a regular communication rhythm.

Teams can also establish internal milestones for tasks within a phase. For a sandwich-shop improvement project, the Measure phase might include internal milestones such as:

- Create definitions of terms: January 25
- Collect temperature data: February 5
- Observe and time sandwich preparation: February 10

The final Measure milestone with the sponsor occurs on February 12. Breaking phases into smaller, manageable pieces with own deadlines keeps the team aligned, makes work feel less overwhelming, and provides multiple check-ins for course correction.

## Budget Communication and Oversight

Project budgets demand continuous, transparent communication. While end customers judge success by performance and quality, corporate leadership also measures success in terms of time and cost. A strong timeline and well-placed milestones help meet time requirements, but budget adherence requires an understanding of financial drivers, honest conversations, and careful oversight.

Two common challenges arise. First, not every team member may be aware of all financial drivers because sensitive pay data cannot be shared openly; in such cases a Black Belt or designated financial person must handle certain analyses confidentially. Second, organizations calculate project budgets differently: some track only direct expenditures like equipment and software, while others also account for team hours and implementation expenses outside the team. The Six Sigma leader must therefore engage in a thorough discussion with the sponsor before budgeting begins to clarify how costs are tallied, what flexibility exists for budget increases, and what role the leader will play in budget maintenance.

When team members understand the financial constraints, they can propose improvements that fit within those limits. A solution requiring an $80,000 capital investment cannot be pursued if the project budget is only $50,000. Clear communication about budgetary realities prevents wasted effort and keeps the team’s proposals feasible.

## Defining Measures of Success

A well-defined measure of success is a crucial communication tool that prevents scope creep, endless projects, and misaligned outcomes. The team, sponsor, and champions must agree on what success looks like. Without this agreement, the team may believe the project finished successfully while leadership considers it a failure. A vague definition of success allows the project to drift, never feeling complete. The team leader must facilitate a dialogue during the Define phase to lock in the specific metrics, targets, or conditions that will signal a successful conclusion. This shared understanding is then referenced at every milestone meeting, and any proposed change in scope or success criteria must be renegotiated with the same stakeholders.

## Exam Tips

- Know the difference between a raw phase-based timeline (quick, based on allotted weeks, phases overlap) and the critical path method (detailed, activity-level, requires team input, identifies the longest sequential chain).
- Remember the six steps for constructing a critical path diagram; be ready to apply them to a simple scenario, identify the critical path among parallel activities, and compute the total duration.
- Understand that milestone meetings are typically scheduled at the end of each DMAIC phase to communicate progress to the sponsor, while internal milestones can be set for sub-tasks within a phase.
- When a question describes a budget conflict, recall that the team must be aware of the financial limits and that the Six Sigma leader must clarify with the sponsor how budgets are calculated.
- A defined measure of success, agreed upon by all stakeholders, prevents scope creep and ensures the project ends when the customer and leadership are satisfied.
