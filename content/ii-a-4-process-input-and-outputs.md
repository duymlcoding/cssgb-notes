## Systems Thinking and Process Inputs and Outputs

Systems thinking is the recognition and consideration of all the individual elements that interrelate with a common purpose toward the whole function of a unit. In a Six Sigma project, systems thinking demands understanding that a change made in one part of an operation can cause new problems somewhere downstream. For example, if a supplier ships a material with a higher than normal moisture content, every process that uses that product must adjust to ensure the end product still meets requirements. This perspective forms the foundation for analyzing process inputs and outputs.

Process inputs are the materials, components, information, energy, or service requests that enter a process. Outputs are the products, services, or information that leave the process and are delivered to customers. Clearly defining inputs and outputs early in the Define phase prevents suboptimization: improving one step while unknowingly harming another. When teams map inputs and outputs systematically, they can trace how input variation propagates through the process and affects outputs, enabling targeted improvement efforts.

## Using SIPOC to Map Inputs and Outputs

A SIPOC diagram is a high-level process map that identifies Suppliers, Inputs, Process, Outputs, and Customers. The Council for Six Sigma Certification Training Manual provides a template that also includes Enablers, which are not consumed by the process but make it possible. An example of an enabler is a conveyor machine that moves items between workstations; it enables efficient flow but is not a direct input.

Creating a SIPOC diagram follows a structured sequence.

1.  **Define the high-level process boundaries.** Identify the start and stop points of the process under study.
2.  **List the primary outputs.** Determine what the process produces, whether a physical product, a service, or information.
3.  **Identify the customers who receive each output.** Customers can be internal (the next process step) or external (the paying end user). Social responsibility stakeholders such as local communities and government agencies may also be customers of the process.
4.  **List the inputs required to produce those outputs.** Inputs are the items that are transformed or consumed.
5.  **Identify the suppliers who provide each input.** Suppliers can be internal or external to the organization.
6.  **Record enablers.** List equipment, systems, or infrastructure that support the process without becoming part of the output.

The SIPOC diagram immediately highlights potential sources of variation. A Six Sigma team can look at an input such as a component from a supplier and ask how its variability affects downstream assembly steps and final product quality. The SIPOC also clarifies how the process contributes to overall business performance by connecting outputs to customer satisfaction.

## Analyzing Input-Output Relationships at the Analyze Level

At the Analyze level of Bloom’s taxonomy, you must go beyond simply listing inputs and outputs. You must examine the relationships among them using systems thinking. For instance, if an upstream process step increases temperature, what adjustment is needed in a downstream curing step to maintain output dimensions? The SIPOC diagram becomes a tool for asking such questions systematically.

Process owners, who have responsibility for the definition, execution, maintenance, and improvement of the process, know these linkages best. Stakeholders, including operators, maintenance personnel, and internal suppliers, are often the most aware of unintended consequences when an input changes. Their involvement when constructing the SIPOC ensures that the team captures realistic dependencies. The ASQ Handbook notes that teams representing process owners and stakeholders have the best knowledge base about the process and the best ideas for improvement. When analyzing input-output relationships, engage these individuals to validate assumptions about what matters and how much variation is acceptable.

The concept of the critical-to-quality (CTQ) tree, while detailed in later phases, begins with understanding which outputs are critical to customers. Mapping outputs to customers in the SIPOC is the first step in a CTQ analysis. The team can later break down each critical output into measurable quality characteristics and link them back to the specific inputs that drive them.

## Exam Tips

- Expect exam questions that ask you to classify an item as a supplier, input, process step, output, or customer within a given scenario. Distinguish between inputs (consumed or transformed) and enablers (facilitators like equipment that are not consumed).
- The CSSGB exam tests your ability to apply systems thinking. Be prepared to predict downstream effects when an input variable changes. The moisture content example from the Handbook is a representative scenario.
- Understand that process owners are accountable for the inputs, outputs, and execution of the process. Questions may ask who has the best knowledge of how a change in an input will affect downstream outputs.
- SIPOC is a tool used in the Define phase to set project boundaries and identify all stakeholders. The exam may ask why a SIPOC is created before data collection begins: it helps scope the project and prevents overlooking key customers and suppliers.
