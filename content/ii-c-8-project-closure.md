## Audit as a Project Closure Activity

A Six Sigma project does not end when the solution is implemented. The closure phase verifies that the new process is stable, the root causes have been removed, and the gains will last. The ASQ CSSGB Handbook describes an audit as a sequence of processes that can serve exactly this purpose. An internal audit, the type most likely used for Six Sigma, provides a structured way to examine the work and confirm that corrective and preventive actions have been effective. The project team or an independent internal auditor follows four phases: planning, performance, reporting, and closure.

### Audit Phases Applied to Project Closure

- **Audit planning**, The auditor prepares by becoming familiar with the process under review, the associated documentation, and the relevant standards. During project closure, this step ensures the auditor understands the baseline, the implemented changes, and the control plan.
- **Audit performance**, The auditor gathers evidence through interviews, observations, and document review. The auditor reconciles any potential nonconformances or opportunities for improvement to validate findings.
- **Audit reporting**, The auditor drafts a report summarizing the scope, activities, and results. The report documents any corrective actions needed (findings) and highlights preventive actions (opportunities for improvement) that could strengthen the solution.
- **Audit closure**, The auditor conducts follow-up activities to verify that action plans have been implemented and are effectively eliminating the root causes. This phase directly supports the goal of project closure: confirming that the process will not slide back toward old performance levels.

### Auditor Conduct for a Project Closure Audit

When a Green Belt participates in an audit team during project closure, the Handbook provides specific guidance. The auditor must be pleasant, treating the audit as a learning activity rather than a fault-finding mission. Preparation is essential; the auditor should review the process, the control plan, and all referenced documents ahead of time. Observations must be factual and thorough, because missing a weakness could harm customer satisfaction or safety. Asking clarifying questions helps keep the assessment constructive. Every observation gets recorded so that the next auditor or process owner has a clear record. The auditor follows the direction of the lead auditor, which is particularly important when the closure audit involves multiple departments.

## Communicating Results with Bar Charts

Project closure also requires clear communication of results. The CSSC Training Manual demonstrates how to create a bar chart that summarizes data effectively. This tool allows stakeholders to see at a glance whether the project met its goals.

The manual uses a dataset of calls per hour as an example. The table contains three hours and their corresponding call counts:

| Hour    | Calls |
|---------|-------|
| 7:00 PM | 198   |
| 8:00 PM | 145   |
| 9:00 PM | 57    |

Follow these steps in Excel to produce the chart:

1.  Highlight the columns that contain the data numbers and their labels (the hour labels and the call counts).
2.  Select **Insert > Chart > Bar Chart**. The simplest bar chart form is appropriate for this data.
3.  Use the formatting tools to customize the chart title, colors, and labels so that the visual is clear and professional.

Most Excel versions offer several chart formats. The vertical display that many people call a bar chart is technically a column chart. A true bar chart shows horizontal bars. The same data can be displayed either way. The manual provides a horizontal bar chart for the call data, which is simply a rotated version of the column chart. The choice depends on the story the data tells and the audience’s preference.

In a project closure presentation, a similar chart might compare defect counts before and after the improvement, or show the reduction in cycle time across shifts. The bar chart distills a large amount of evidence into a single, easy-to-interpret image.

## Why Closure Matters in a Six Sigma Project

Project closure is not a formality. Without a deliberate verification step, improvements can erode. The audit structure provides an objective, repeatable method to check that every action plan has become a routine part of daily work. The bar chart ensures that the data behind those checks is visible to everyone, from operators to sponsors. Together, these tools give the Green Belt the means to declare a project complete with confidence.

## Exam Tips

- Expect a question that asks you to match a description to an audit phase. For example, you may need to recognize that verifying implementation of action plans occurs during audit closure, not during reporting or performance.
- Know that internal audits are the type used in Six Sigma environments, and be able to recall the auditor conduct points: be pleasant, prepared, factual, thorough, ask questions, record observations, and follow the lead auditor.
- The exam may ask you to distinguish a column chart from a bar chart or to identify the correct sequence of steps to create a chart from a given table. Practice highlighting data, selecting Insert > Chart > Bar Chart, and applying basic formatting.
- Look for scenario questions that place you in a project closure audit. Apply the four-phase sequence to decide what the auditor should do next, such as reconciling an observation of noncompliance before writing a finding.
- A numerical question could ask you to interpret a bar chart that displays before-and-after project data. Interpret the heights or lengths of bars without overcomplicating the display. The key is linking the visual to the project outcome.
