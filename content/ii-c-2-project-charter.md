## What Is a Project Charter?

A project charter is a short document, typically limited to one or two pages, that serves as the top-level quick overview of a Six Sigma project. Its brevity is intentional: the charter gives sponsors, team members, and other stakeholders a clear, shared understanding of the project's boundaries, purpose, and expected outcomes without requiring them to read lengthy plans. The charter is a required deliverable of the Define phase. The define tollgate checklist explicitly calls for "a team charter" as one of the items that must be completed before a project can move into the Measure phase.

## Components of the Project Charter

The charter template from the Council for Six Sigma Certification manual identifies the essential sections that a complete project charter must contain.

**Project Name**  
A clear, descriptive title that immediately communicates the subject of the improvement effort.

**Team Members**  
This section lists the project sponsor and each team member, along with their role on the project and the percentage of time they are expected to commit. Listing the sponsor ensures that executive accountability is documented from the start.

**Critical-to-Quality Characteristics (CTQs)**  
CTQs translate the voice of the customer into specific, measurable attributes that the project must deliver. They are the performance characteristics that most directly affect customer satisfaction.

**Financial Drivers**  
This part of the charter captures the expected financial benefit of the project. Documenting financial drivers links the improvement work to concrete business results and helps justify the resources being requested.

**Customers and Stakeholders**  
The charter records internal customers, external customers, and non-customer stakeholders. Internal customers are those within the organization who receive the process output. External customers are the end users or paying customers. Non-customer stakeholders have an interest in the project but are not direct recipients of the process output; they might include regulatory bodies, suppliers, or other departments indirectly affected.

**In Scope and Out of Scope**  
Defining what is included in the project and what is explicitly excluded is one of the most important functions of the charter. Clear scope boundaries prevent scope creep and keep the team focused on a manageable set of activities. Using a tool like the "in and out of the box" method or an is/is not matrix, the team agrees on these limits before the charter is finalized.

**Problem Statement**  
The problem statement concisely describes the gap between current performance and desired performance. It should be specific, measurable, and phrased so that the team and sponsors understand exactly what issue the project will address. A good problem statement provides a clear starting point for data collection and root cause analysis.

**Objective or Goal**  
This section defines what success will look like. The goal should be measurable and aligned with the CTQs and financial drivers. The project's objective must be agreed upon by the team members and any sponsors or executive leaders, as specified in the define tollgate checklist. Without this shared agreement, the project has no common destination.

**Project Schedule**  
The charter includes a high-level timeline showing major milestones. This schedule is not a detailed project plan but rather a summary that communicates when key phases and reviews will occur. It gives stakeholders confidence that the project is planned and that progress can be tracked.

## Why the Charter Matters in a Six Sigma Project

The charter operates as a contract among the team, the sponsor, and the organization. Because it is short, it is read and referenced frequently. The charter aligns everyone on the project's scope, goals, and resource commitments before significant time is invested. When disagreements arise about whether an activity belongs in the project, the team refers back to the in-scope and out-of-scope statements to make a quick, consistent decision. The charter also serves as a communication tool for stakeholders who are not deeply involved in the day-to-day work; they can review the document to receive an accurate snapshot of the project at a glance.

Completing the charter is a collaborative process. The team must work together to populate each section, often using brainstorming techniques to identify risks, stakeholders, and scope elements. Once approved, the charter becomes the foundational document that guides the project through the remaining DMAIC phases.

## Exam Tips

- Recognize that a project charter is a concise document. Exam questions may test whether you know that the charter is limited to one or two pages and is not a detailed project plan.
- Be prepared to identify missing elements in a partially completed charter scenario. Common gaps tested include absent scope boundaries, missing problem statements, or unclear CTQs.
- Understand that the charter requires agreement from both the team and sponsors. A scenario might ask what to do if the sponsor has not signed off on the goal; the correct answer will involve securing that agreement before proceeding.
- Know that the define phase tollgate cannot be passed without a completed team charter. Questions may ask what key deliverables mark the completion of Define.
- When choosing between project documents in a scenario, remember that the charter is the top-level overview. Detailed schedules, risk matrices, and measurement plans live in the project management plan or specific phase deliverables, not in the charter itself.
