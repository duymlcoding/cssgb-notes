## Overview

A process capability study determines whether a process can consistently meet customer specifications. Before calculating any capability index, the practitioner must confirm that the measurement system is adequate, the process is stable (free of special causes), and the individual data values follow an approximately normal distribution. The study then uses the process average and estimated standard deviation to quantify the proportion of output that falls outside the specification limits.

## Identifying Characteristics, Specifications, and Tolerances

Only a few characteristics typically justify a full capability study. The practitioner selects those that past experience has shown to be difficult to hold to specification. Customers may designate critical characteristics that require statistical process control (SPC). The best practice is to perform a comprehensive Process Failure Mode and Effects Analysis (FMEA), identify the parameters that require SPC, and create a control plan with detailed SPC planning. This disciplined selection avoids wasting resources on monitoring characteristics that already perform well.

Specifications and tolerances come from engineering drawings, customer contracts, or public guarantees. In service industries, providers identify specifications and tolerances through surveys and interviews because customers rarely state them explicitly. Examples include next-day delivery, wait-time guarantees of “15 minutes or free,” or emergency maintenance resolved within 24 hours.

## Steps for Conducting a Process Capability Study

### 1. Measurement System Verification

Measurement system analysis (MSA) must be performed first. Variation from the measurement system can distort the capability assessment and the control chart signals. The goal is to reduce the percentage of measurement error to a small proportion of the overall process variation and of the tolerance width. Only after the measurement system is acceptable does the team proceed to the next steps.

### 2. Rational Subgrouping and Control Charting

Select a rational subgrouping scheme so that within-subgroup variation contains only common causes, while between-subgroup variation captures special causes. Typical subgroup sizes range from two to ten, with five being the most common. Consecutive parts sampled at equal intervals provide the data for an $\bar{X}$ and $R$ chart. For subgroup sizes greater than eight, the $\bar{X}$ and $s$ chart is used because it is more powerful at detecting shifts, though data collection costs may be higher. For low-volume processes, individual and moving range charts with a subgroup size of one are appropriate, but they are less sensitive to process shifts.

### 3. Verifying Stability

Plot at least 20 subgroups on the appropriate control chart. If no points fall outside the control limits and no patterns of special causes appear, the process is considered stable. While authorities debate the exact number, 20 to 30 subgroups are commonly required. A stable process is equivalent to the absence of assignable-cause variation. Control charts remain valid even when the underlying data distribution is nonnormal, because the central limit theorem makes the charting statistics robust. However, normality of the individual measurements must be checked before capability estimates are calculated.

### 4. Verifying Normality

Construct a histogram using all the individual readings from the control chart (not the subgroup averages). The histogram should show a single peak with fairly symmetric tails. A probability plot with confidence intervals, generated by commercial statistical software, provides a more rigorous normality test. If the data are not normally distributed, transformations such as Box-Cox or Johnson transformations can convert them to an approximately normal shape so that standard normal distribution tables may be used.

### 5. Estimating Process Capability Using Z-Values and Natural Process Limits

Once stability and normality are confirmed, the process average $\bar{X}$ and estimated standard deviation $\hat{s}$ from the control chart are used to evaluate capability. The number of standard deviations between the process average and each specification limit is computed.

$$
Z_U = \frac{USL - \bar{X}}{\hat{s}}
$$
where
$USL$ = upper specification limit,  
$\bar{X}$ = process average,  
$\hat{s}$ = estimated standard deviation.

$$
Z_L = \frac{\bar{X} - LSL}{\hat{s}}
$$
where
$LSL$ = lower specification limit.

The area beyond each Z-value in a standard normal curve table represents the proportion of production falling outside that specification limit.

**Example 1: Package Shipping Weight**

A shipping process has $USL = 1.10$ kg, $LSL = 1.00$ kg, $\bar{X} = 1.065$ kg, and $\hat{s} = 0.024$ kg.

Compute $Z_U$:
$$
Z_U = \frac{1.10 - 1.065}{0.024} = \frac{0.035}{0.024} \approx 1.46
$$

Compute $Z_L$:
$$
Z_L = \frac{1.065 - 1.00}{0.024} = \frac{0.065}{0.024} \approx 2.70
$$

From the standard normal table, the area beyond $Z = 1.46$ is 0.0721 (7.21% overweight), and the area beyond $Z = 2.70$ is 0.0035 (0.35% underweight). The total predicted nonconformance is approximately 7.56%. The practitioner would conclude that the process is not centered between the specification limits and that the long upper tail dominates the nonconformance. If $Z_U$ had been 2.0 instead of 1.46, the overweight proportion would drop to about 2.3%, and the team might focus first on reducing variation rather than shifting the mean.

**Example 2: Certification Exam Scores**

A certification test requires a score between 70 and 100 to pass. A random sample of 15 scores from a training program gives $\bar{X} = 85.2$ and $\hat{s} = 9.987$ (sample standard deviation). Treat these as estimates of the process parameters.

$$
Z_U = \frac{100 - 85.2}{9.987} = \frac{14.8}{9.987} \approx 1.48
$$
$$
Z_L = \frac{85.2 - 70}{9.987} = \frac{15.2}{9.987} \approx 1.52
$$

Using a standard normal table, the area beyond 1.48 is approximately 0.0694, and the area beyond 1.52 is approximately 0.0643. The combined fraction outside specifications is about 13.4%. This relatively symmetric distribution still has a substantial failure rate because the spread is large relative to the tolerance. If the standard deviation were reduced to 7, both Z-values would exceed 2.1, cutting the total nonconformance below 4%. The practitioner would prioritize variation reduction over mean adjustment.

**Natural Process Limits**

The traditional definition of natural process limits is $\bar{X} \pm 3\hat{s}$. These limits indicate where nearly all (99.73%) of the individual measurements will fall if the process remains stable. If the natural process limits extend beyond the specification limits, the process is not capable of meeting the specifications.

For the shipping example:
$$
\text{Natural limits} = 1.065 \pm 3(0.024) = 0.993 \text{ to } 1.137 \text{ kg}
$$
These exceed the specification band of 1.00–1.10 kg, confirming that the process will regularly produce units outside the tolerance. If the natural limits had fallen entirely inside [1.00, 1.10], the process would be deemed capable even without centering.

After estimating the proportion nonconforming, the team acts to improve capability. The first step is to center the process, because shifting the mean when the spread is narrower than the tolerance can quickly reduce the defect rate. If centering is insufficient, the team reduces the variation. In some economic situations, the mean is deliberately off-centered toward a specification limit that results in rework rather than scrap, serving as a containment action until variation is reduced.

## Sampling Methods for SPC Data Collection

Three sampling strategies are used to collect data for control charts and capability studies.

**Random Sampling** is appropriate for batch processes such as oven baking, spray painting, or heat treatment. If earlier experiments confirm that a randomly selected part represents the entire batch, the measurements from several random parts are averaged to form one subgroup point. Batch-to-batch variation is then tracked with a moving range of the averages, while within-batch variation is monitored via a range or standard deviation chart.

**Systematic Sampling** involves selecting every $n$th part from a conveyor line or every $n$th customer transaction. This method provides a uniform time-based sampling and is typical for individual and moving range charts.

**Subgroup Sampling** requires that consecutive parts be taken at a fixed interval to form each subgroup. The within-subgroup variation should contain only common causes, making the $\bar{X}$ and $R$ chart effective at detecting special causes that appear between subgroups. The subgroup intervals must be chosen to capture any special causes that may arise. For example, a typical practice is to take five consecutive parts every hour.

## Practical Considerations

Organizations sometimes create internal specifications that are tighter than customer specifications to provide extra protection to the customer. Process capability studies often uncover specifications that were set unrealistically tight by designers without considering the actual process capability; revisiting these specifications can be a legitimate improvement action. If the process data are nonnormal and transformation fails, alternative methods such as calculating limits appropriate for the specific distribution (for example, percentiles from a fitted distribution) or using nonnormal capability analysis may be required.

## Exam Tips

1. Given a stable process with known $\bar{X}$, $\hat{s}$, USL, and LSL, compute $Z_U$ and $Z_L$ and use a standard normal table snippet to determine the fraction of product outside specifications. Be prepared to explain which specification limit is more problematic and why.
2. Differentiate between the conditions required for control charting (stability, rational subgrouping) and those required for capability estimation (normality of individuals). Explain why a process can be monitored with control charts even when data are nonnormal but capability assessment demands normality.
3. Order the steps of a capability study correctly: MSA, rational subgrouping, control chart plotting, stability check, normality verification, and finally capability estimation with Z-values or natural process limits.
4. Interpret natural process limits ($\bar{X} \pm 3\hat{s}$) relative to specification limits. Judge whether a process is capable simply by comparing the two intervals, without calculating an index.
5. Select the correct sampling method (random, systematic, subgroup) when given a description of a production scenario, and defend the choice based on how special and common cause variation must be segregated.
6. Critique a scenario where a process capability study is conducted without verifying measurement system adequacy or without checking normality. Predict the likely consequences for the validity of the capability conclusions.
