## Purpose of Project Documentation in Define

During the Define phase, the Six Sigma team creates a shared, written understanding of what the project will accomplish and how success will be measured. This documentation prevents scope creep, aligns stakeholders, and provides a touchstone for every subsequent DMAIC phase. The Define tollgate checklist requires a documented output, and the primary vehicle is the team charter. Additional tools such as the In and Out of the Box method and the Is/Is Not matrix produce documentation that feeds directly into the charter’s scope sections.

## Define Tollgate Deliverables

According to the Council for Six Sigma Certification, a successful Define phase produces documentation for all of the following items:

- A comprehensive problem statement
- A team charter
- An understanding of the process, along with a project diagram or map
- An understanding of the Voice of the Customer
- A definition of what success looks like, agreed upon by the team, sponsors, and executive leaders

These deliverables become the formal record of the project’s direction. The problem statement and the definition of success are integrated into the team charter.

## The Team Charter

The team charter is the central document of the Define phase. The charter template includes the following elements. Each element must be completed with specific, unambiguous information.

**Project Name** – A short, descriptive title that team members and stakeholders can easily reference.

**Team Members and Sponsor** – Lists the sponsor (an executive with resource authority) and every team member. The template includes columns for Name, Role, and Time Commitment. Documenting time commitment sets expectations about availability and ensures leadership support.

**CTQs (Critical to Quality)** – The key customer requirements that the project must satisfy. CTQs translate the Voice of the Customer into measurable characteristics. The charter lists these explicitly.

**Financial Drivers** – The economic justification for the project. Documenting expected cost savings, revenue increases, or cost avoidance ties the project to business outcomes and aids prioritization.

**Internal Customers** – Groups or individuals within the organization who receive the process output or are affected by the project outcome. Listing them ensures that internal requirements are not overlooked.

**External Customers** – End users or outside parties who purchase, use, or depend on the product or service. Separation from internal customers clarifies whose voice carries weight in different situations.

**Non-Customer Stakeholders** – Individuals or groups who do not directly consume the process output but have influence or interest. Examples include procurement managers, compliance officers, or facilities personnel. The charter distinguishes them from customers so the team can manage communication appropriately.

**In Scope** – A concise, bulleted list of what the team is authorized to work on. The scope documentation sets boundaries for data collection, root cause analysis, and solution implementation.

**Out of Scope** – Elements explicitly excluded from the project. Documenting out-of-scope items gives the team permission to say no when requests drift beyond the charter.

**Problem Statement** – A clear, fact-based description of the gap between current performance and the desired state. It should include where the problem occurs, what it is, and when it was first observed. An Is/Is Not matrix frequently provides the raw data for this statement.

**Objective / Goal** – A statement of what the team intends to achieve, phrased in measurable terms. It defines the target metric and often includes a deadline.

**Project Schedule** – A high-level timeline showing major milestones or tollgates. The schedule documents the anticipated pace of the project and helps stakeholders plan reviews.

### Creating the In-Scope and Out-of-Scope Documentation

Teams use two structured methods to generate the content that populates the scope sections of the charter.

**In and Out of the Box Method**

1. Draw a large box on a whiteboard or flip chart.
2. Distribute markers and sticky notes to the team.
3. Instruct the team to write down every element of the process: resources, activities, people. Place all sticky notes on a wall or table without discussion.
4. Once a comprehensive list exists, work as a team to assign each sticky note to one of three areas – inside the box (in scope), on the line (uncertain), or outside the box (out of scope). Items go outside the box when the team lacks access, control, time, or permission. Items on the line require the team leader to seek permission or assistance.
5. Review all placements as a group and make adjustments. Document the final arrangement by photographing the diagram or recreating it digitally.

This method produces a visual scope record that the team can reference when scope creep arises.

**Is/Is Not Matrix**

1. Create a table with columns “Is” and “Is Not.”
2. Ask structured questions about the project, such as “Where does the problem occur?”, “What is affected?”, and “When did it start?”
3. Populate the “Is” column with relevant answers and the “Is Not” column with corresponding exclusions. For example, a furnace project might list “South plant” under Is and “North or East plant” under Is Not, “Steam furnaces” under Is and “Wood furnaces” under Is Not, “January 2015” under Is and “Prior to January 2015” under Is Not.
4. Use the completed matrix to draft crisp scope statements for the charter.

Both tools document the boundary decisions the team makes, and that documentation prevents later misunderstandings.

## Why Project Documentation Matters

Thorough project documentation forces the team to convert assumptions into explicit agreements. When the charter lists CTQs, financial drivers, and scope, sponsors can see exactly what they are funding. The documented problem statement and objective give the team a single, shared target. When a project’s scope is documented with methods like the In and Out of the Box and the Is/Is Not matrix, the team has concrete evidence to resist scope creep or to request additional resources when new demands appear. During tollgate reviews, the charter serves as the benchmark against which progress is evaluated.

## Exam Tips

- The CSSGB exam expects you to identify the key elements of a project charter: problem statement, scope, team members, sponsor, CTQs, financial drivers, and schedule. Be able to match each element to its purpose.
- For apply-level questions, expect a scenario describing a project’s boundary confusion. You must select the correct tool (In and Out of the Box, Is/Is Not matrix) and describe how to use it to document scope.
- Understand that the problem statement captures the “Is/Is Not” distinctions (where, what, when) to narrow the project focus. A weak problem statement that includes opinions or vague phrases is a common trick.
- The charter is a living document created during Define but referenced throughout DMAIC. Exam questions may ask when the charter is finalized or approved: it must be agreed upon by the team, sponsor, and executives before the team proceeds to Measure.
- Documentation of “what success looks like” is not generic; it must be measurable and linked to the CTQs and financial drivers. Expect questions that test whether a given goal meets that standard.
