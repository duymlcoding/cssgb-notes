## Project Scope in the Define Phase

Defining project scope early in the Define phase sets clear boundaries around the work the team will perform. A well-scoped project prevents scope creep, focuses resources on the elements the team can realistically influence, and aligns stakeholders on what is included and what is excluded. Two rapid, collaborative tools help teams achieve this: the In and Out of the Box method and the Is/Is Not Matrix. The final scope is then documented in the team charter.

### In and Out of the Box Method

The In and Out of the Box method lets a team visually separate process elements into in-scope, out-of-scope, and uncertain categories. It relies on brainstorming without early criticism to capture a complete picture. Follow this procedure step by step.

1. Draw a large box on a whiteboard or flip chart.
2. Provide markers and sticky notes to all team members.
3. Ask the team to write down every element of the process or project on individual sticky notes. Elements include resources, activities, and people. During this phase, discussion is limited to encourage the broadest possible list.
4. As a team, place each sticky note in one of three positions:
   - **Inside the box:** Items the team can reasonably influence and that are in scope.
   - **On the line of the box:** Items the team hopes to impact but for which the team leader must first seek permission or assistance. These are not yet confirmed as in scope.
   - **Outside the box:** Items the team will not address because they lack control, time, or specific instruction to exclude them.
5. Review every placement together. Move sticky notes if needed until the team agrees on the final boundary.
6. Document the completed diagram with a photograph or by re-creating it in a computer application.

The team can refer back to the diagram throughout the project to control scope creep and to identify people outside the team whose help may be required.

### Is/Is Not Matrix

The Is/Is Not Matrix provides a structured way to narrow scope by asking specific questions about the process and recording both what it *is* and what it *is not*. This tool also helps build key portions of a problem statement. Begin by selecting categories such as *Where*, *What*, *When*, *Who*, or *How*. For each category, fill in the “Is” and “Is Not” answers based on the available information.

Consider a Six Sigma team tasked with determining why the furnaces in a certain factory are not heating to proper temperatures. They might create an Is/Is Not Matrix like the one below.

```{list-table}
:header-rows: 1

* - Category
  - Is
  - Is Not
* - Where
  - South plant
  - North or East plant
* - What
  - Steam furnaces
  - Wood furnaces
* - When
  - January 2015
  - Prior to January 2015
```

This matrix instantly defines that the scope includes only the steam furnaces at the south plant. The problem was first noted in January 2015, which gives the team a starting point for data collection. Teams can add as many rows as needed to further refine the boundary.

### Scope in the Team Charter

After the team reaches agreement using one or both tools, the final scope is recorded in the team charter. The charter template explicitly includes fields for *In Scope* and *Out of Scope*. Capturing the boundaries there ensures that team members, sponsors, and key players share a common understanding of what the project will and will not address.

## Exam Tips

- Given a scenario, you must be able to facilitate an In and Out of the Box session: identify which items belong inside, on, or outside the box, and explain that items placed on the line require follow-up with a sponsor or resource owner before they become in-scope.
- Construct an Is/Is Not Matrix from a brief problem description. Choose appropriate categories (for example, location, product type, time frame) and fill in both “Is” and “Is Not” columns to define the boundary and pin down a starting point for measurement.
- Recognize that the Is/Is Not Matrix directly supports writing a problem statement; the exam may ask you to extract scope information from a matrix and use it to create the problem statement.
- Know that the scope definition is one of the required tollgate deliverables for the Define phase and must be documented in the team charter. Expect questions that test how a team uses these scope documents to prevent scope creep during later phases.
- Differentiate the two tools: use In and Out of the Box when the team needs to brainstorm tangible elements (resources, activities, people) rapidly, and use the Is/Is Not Matrix when the team must narrow the investigation with categorical distinctions.
