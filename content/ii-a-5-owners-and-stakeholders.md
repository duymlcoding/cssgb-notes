## Understanding Stakeholders and Owners in Six Sigma

Stakeholders are the interest points that generate requirements for a process, product, or service. The Council for Six Sigma Certification’s 2018 training manual groups these interest points into three explicit categories: customer expectations, compliance and regulatory rules, and brand expectations set by in-house leadership. Every one of those categories represents a stakeholder group whose needs must be captured, analyzed, and translated into measurable project goals. In a Six Sigma project, failing to identify a stakeholder means missing a requirement. Missing a requirement leads directly to a process that underperforms, a product that disappoints, or a compliance failure that carries legal or financial consequences.

The distinction between an “owner” and a general stakeholder is operational. A process owner is the internal stakeholder who holds authority over the process and is accountable for its performance. The training manual describes in-house leadership that articulates brand expectations; that leadership is essentially a set of process owners who define non-negotiable attributes such as a brand voice, a visual identity, or a quality standard that must be met even when customers or regulators do not demand them. In practice, process owners are the individuals who will sustain the improvements after the project team disbands. Identifying them early ensures that project metrics align with what the organization, not just the external customer, considers valuable.

### Identifying Stakeholders with the SIPOC Model

A SIPOC diagram is one of the first tools a Six Sigma team uses to map stakeholders. The template directly labels “Suppliers” and “Customers” as distinct boxes. Suppliers provide the inputs that the process consumes; they are stakeholders because any change to a process may alter what is demanded from them or how they deliver. Customers receive the outputs; they are the stakeholders who ultimately judge whether quality has been achieved. The process box itself implies internal stakeholders, the people who operate and own the steps.

When a team fills in a SIPOC, they are answering a critical question: “Who has a stake in this process?” Suppliers and customers are the most visible. However, the 2018 manual also notes that requirements are generated by different interest points. A SIPOC exercise should therefore prompt the team to look beyond immediate commercial customers and ask whether a regulatory body (obligatory requirements) or an internal brand owner (stated expectations) must appear as a customer or as an input provider. A bank processing credit card payments, for instance, must list the Payment Card Industry Data Security Standards council as a stakeholder that imposes security requirements on the process. The SIPOC gives that regulatory stakeholder a concrete place in the system view.

### Translating Stakeholder Requirements through QFD Weighting

Once stakeholders are identified, the Define phase requires the team to translate their often-qualitative statements into technical requirements that can be acted upon. The ASQ CSSGB Handbook prescribes Quality Function Deployment (QFD), also called the House of Quality, as the primary mechanism. The input to QFD is the Voice of the Customer (VOC), which aggregates the needs and desires of all relevant stakeholders. The QFD matrix then links those customer requirements to the technical requirements the organization must deliver.

Weighting methods become essential because stakeholders do not speak with one uniform voice. The handbook explicitly states: “Use weighting methods as needed to amplify the importance and urgency of different kinds of input; telephone call vs. survey response; product complaint vs. expedited service request.” A complaint from a key account arguably carries more urgency than a single survey checkbox. The weighting approach built into the QFD matrix applies numerical values to the relationships between customer requirements and technical requirements, turning stakeholder priorities into directly comparable numbers.

The standard relationship scale inside the QFD uses arbitrary but consistent values: strong relationship = 9, moderate = 3, weak = 1. The customer requirements section includes an importance rating (for example, 1 to 5, with 5 being most critical). To calculate the column weight for a technical requirement, multiply the importance of each customer requirement by the relationship value assigned to that technical requirement, then sum across all customer requirements that have a relationship. This number tells the team which technical requirement most powerfully satisfies the weighted stakeholder demands.

Below is a simple numerical example that applies the method with one customer requirement to show the computation step-by-step.

- Customer requirement: “Delivery speed” with an importance score of 5.
- Technical requirement A: “Order processing cycle time” shows a strong relationship to delivery speed, assigned a value of 9.
- Technical requirement B: “Packaging and labeling time” shows a moderate relationship, assigned 3.

The column weight for technical requirement A is: \$5 \times 9 = 45$.  
The column weight for technical requirement B is: \$5 \times 3 = 15$.

The resulting weights, 45 and 15, quantify the fact that improving order processing cycle time is three times more effective at satisfying the stakeholder demand for fast delivery than improving packaging time alone. When multiple customer requirements exist, the same multiplication is performed for each relationship cell and the products are summed down each column. Teams use these column weights to prioritize technical design efforts, allocate resources, and justify decisions to the process owners and other stakeholders who will sign off on the Define phase tollgate.

## Exam Tips

- On the CSSGB exam, expect to see a SIPOC diagram and be asked which boxes represent stakeholders: Suppliers and Customers are the explicit stakeholder positions; the Process box implies the process owner.
- Recognize the three stakeholder requirement categories from the 2018 CSSC manual: customer expectations (stated or implied), compliance/regulatory rules (obligatory), and brand expectations from in-house leadership. A question may ask which category a given requirement falls into.
- Be prepared to apply the QFD weighting calculation using the standard relationship values (9, 3, 1) and a given importance rating. You may be asked to compute a single column weight or to identify which technical requirement has the highest total weight.
- Understand that different types of stakeholder input (complaint, survey response, phone call) may be assigned different weights to amplify urgency. The exam may present a scenario where you must decide which input should receive the greatest weighting.
- Process owners are internal stakeholders responsible for long-term performance. Questions may test whether you can distinguish between a process owner and an external customer.
