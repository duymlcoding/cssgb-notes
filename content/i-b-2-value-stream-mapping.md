## Overview of Value Stream Mapping

Value stream mapping is a lean tool that visualizes the complete flow of activities required to fulfill a customer order. The ASQ CSSGB Handbook emphasizes that a value stream map must not be limited to internal operations. Instead, it should reach all the way upstream into suppliers’ plants. This extended view reveals where waste hides and where improvements will produce the greatest benefit. The map shows how a customer demand signal travels backward through the value chain and triggers the production of a replacement item before the next customer order requires it.

The Handbook identifies four distinct flows that travel through a value stream: **information**, **material**, **knowledge**, and **money**. Every one of these flows is a legitimate target for lean improvements. Incompatibilities in communication systems between a company and its suppliers can generate delays just as surely as a physical material shortage. By placing all four flows on a single diagram, a value stream map helps a Six Sigma team see coordination gaps, duplicated effort, and unnecessary lag.

## Key Principles from the Source

The source describes a fulfillment scenario in which a customer order is filled from finished goods inventory. The order then triggers upstream activities that produce a replacement item in finished goods inventory before it is needed by a customer. This scenario teaches several principles that value stream mapping makes visible.

First, the approach avoids overproduction. The replacement is triggered only when actual consumption occurs, a pull signal replaces a forecast-driven push. Mapping the value stream reveals this trigger and shows where inventory piles up or where the pull signal fails to reach an upstream step.

Second, the Handbook explicitly tells practitioners to extend the map into suppliers’ plants. New challenges arise around communication system compatibility when companies connect with external partners. The extended map exposes these handoff points so the team can see where information, material, knowledge, or money stall or become distorted.

Third, the source warns against trying to “boil the ocean.” When beginning a value stream mapping effort, the team must **pick a narrow focus**. A manageable scope might be a single product family, a specific customer segment, or a tightly defined set of processes. A narrow scope keeps the map detailed enough to uncover actionable improvement opportunities without drowning the team in complexity.

## Procedure for Value Stream Mapping

Although the provided source does not enumerate a formal step-by-step methodology, a logical sequence emerges from the guidance.

- **Select a narrow scope.** Start with one product, one customer demand signal, or one segment of the business. Avoid attempting to map the entire enterprise at once.
- **Map the current-state material and information flows.** Begin at the point of customer demand and work backward through the fulfillment process. Include finished goods inventory and the trigger points that signal upstream production.
- **Extend the map upstream into suppliers’ plants.** Document how orders, forecasts, and feedback travel to suppliers. Capture the communication systems used and note any compatibility problems.
- **Identify the four flows.** Explicitly trace how information, material, knowledge, and money move through the value stream. Look for disconnects, delays, and points where the flows diverge or stall.
- **Analyze for waste and improvement opportunities.** Use the map to locate overproduction, inventory accumulations, unnecessary transport, waiting, and other forms of muda. Determine whether replacement items are being produced too early or too late relative to real customer need.
- **Keep the scope manageable.** Review the map for completeness, but resist scope creep. The narrow focus keeps the team’s efforts productive and the analysis sharp.

## Why Value Stream Mapping Matters in Six Sigma

A detailed value stream map provides a baseline for the Measure and Analyze phases of DMAIC. It quantifies where waste exists and points to the vital few inputs that drive defects or delays. The extended map, which includes supplier processes, ensures that improvements do not simply shift a problem from one link of the chain to another. When a team discovers that a communication incompatibility with a supplier is causing late shipments, the solution can address the root cause rather than merely speeding up internal inspections.

The narrow-focus principle aligns directly with Six Sigma’s emphasis on project scoping. A gap that is too broad will not yield statistically meaningful improvements. A well-scoped value stream map, on the other hand, isolates a manageable problem area where data can be collected and a hypothesis can be tested. This tool helps the organization move toward a pull-based, low-waste system that responds directly to customer demand.

## Exam Tips

- Remember that value stream mapping extends **upstream into suppliers** and captures **information, material, knowledge, and money** flows, not just material flow. Exam questions will test this broad scope.
- The **narrow focus** warning is key. “Boil the ocean” is the opposite of good scoping. Choose answers that advocate starting with a manageable slice of the value stream.
- Understand the connection between value stream mapping and pull production. The source example of filling from finished goods and then triggering upstream replenishment shows how mapping reveals the pull signal. Expect questions linking VSM to Lean pull concepts.
- Recognize that the four flows are all improvement targets. A scenario describing a supplier communication breakdown is a value stream mapping opportunity, not just an IT issue.
- At the Understand level, you are not required to draw a map or list specific symbols, but you should recognize that value stream mapping is a tool for identifying waste and scoping improvements during the early stages of a project.
