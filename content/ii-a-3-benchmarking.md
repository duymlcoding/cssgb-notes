## Benchmarking

Benchmarking is the practice of comparing an organization’s processes, products, or services against those of recognized leaders, whether inside the same company or in external organizations. This comparison helps a Six Sigma team set meaningful improvement targets and uncover fresh approaches when usual idea generation stalls. The ASQ CSSGB Handbook emphasizes that benchmarking is especially valuable for driving breakthrough improvement rather than incremental, continuous change.

### Why Benchmarking Matters in Six Sigma

In the Define phase, teams need to establish clear performance expectations. Benchmarking provides a fact-based view of what level of process performance is actually possible. By revealing a gap between current performance and best-in-class results, a team can determine a realistic yet ambitious rate of improvement. This gap also supports the creation of intermediate stretch goals that keep the project focused and energized. Benchmarking reduces the tendency to rely exclusively on ideas generated inside the team; it encourages looking outward for proven solutions. A potential drawback is that benchmarking solely within one’s own industry can trap an organization in second place at best, because it may limit exposure to truly exceptional practices found elsewhere.

### Four Steps of Benchmarking

The Handbook describes a sequence of four steps:

- **Analyze the operation.** Understand the current process, its boundaries, and its performance metrics before looking outward.
- **Know the competition and the industry leaders.** Identify who does this process well, both inside and outside the sector.
- **Incorporate the best of the best.** Adopt or adapt the outstanding practices found during research and site visits.
- **Gain superiority.** Use the adopted practices to move beyond the benchmark and achieve a leading position.

Information for benchmarking can be gathered from publications, professional conferences, university research, customer feedback, site visits, and competitive product analysis. Formal benchmarking exercises often involve detailed planning; the Handbook references specific planning tips in Table 4.1 and Chapter 7.

### Types of Benchmarking

The CSSGB Body of Knowledge expects practitioners to understand several types, each with distinct advantages and limitations.

**Internal benchmarking** compares operations across different departments or divisions within the same company. It offers easy access to data and partners because cooperation is built on existing relationships. The main limitation is that the search for exceptional performance may be constrained by the organization’s own culture, norms, and history.

**Competitive benchmarking** forces an external perspective by looking at direct competitors within the same industry. This approach can reveal where a company stands relative to its rivals. However, if the entire industry does not enjoy a strong reputation for quality, focusing only on competitors may cap the performance level a team can envision.

**Functional benchmarking** compares similar functions but searches outside the organization’s industry. This type provides ample opportunities to identify truly innovative partners because it breaks free of sector-specific blind spots. For example, a hospital admissions process might learn from a hotel check-in system.

**Collaborative benchmarking** involves cooperation among different functions or separate organizations to achieve benchmarking results together. This arrangement can grant access to specific partners that might not be available through internal or competitive routes, fostering shared learning and mutual improvement.

The Handbook illustrates the power of looking beyond one’s industry with a simple example. To benchmark a process that requires fast, accurate order fulfillment with minimal wait (a lean pull system), a team could observe a local McDonald’s restaurant. The quick-service restaurant demonstrates how a process can take an order and deliver exactly what the customer requested within minutes, often using a young, rotating workforce.

## Exam Tips

- The CSSGB exam may ask you to distinguish between competitive and functional benchmarking. Remember: competitive stays within the same industry; functional crosses industry boundaries to examine similar functions.
- Be prepared to identify the major limitation of internal benchmarking: it is bounded by the company’s own culture and history, so it may not reveal truly best-in-class performance.
- Understand that benchmarking is specifically highlighted as a tool for driving breakthrough improvement, not merely continuous incremental gains.
- The four-step sequence (Analyze, Know the competition, Incorporate the best, Gain superiority) appears in the Handbook and may be tested as a logical order.
- Recognize that collaborative benchmarking emphasizes cooperation between entities and can provide access to partners unavailable through other types.
