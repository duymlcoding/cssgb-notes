## Why Communication Matters in the Define Phase

In Six Sigma projects, the Define phase establishes the foundation for all later work. Team members, the project sponsor, and the Black Belt must align on purpose, scope, timing, resources, and the definition of success. Poor communication during this stage leads to the negative team dynamics described in *The ASQ CSSGB Handbook*, including floundering and reluctant participants. Communication is not a soft skill add-on; it is a critical project management discipline that keeps the team focused, ensures realistic expectations, and secures the support needed to execute DMAIC effectively.

## Communication and Negative Team Dynamics

The handbook identifies two common negative dynamics that are rooted in communication breakdowns. Understanding their symptoms, causes, and countermeasures equips a Green Belt to intervene early.

### Floundering
A floundering team proceeds in an unsteady, faltering manner. Symptoms include hesitation, indecision, and vulnerability to external distractions.

**Causes linked to communication**
- Unclear direction from leadership; high-profile leaders rarely attend meetings.
- Organizational upheaval (downsizing, mergers, offshore transfers) leaves the team unsure of its future.
- Postponed decisions because there is no clear direction, making decision-making difficult.

**Communication countermeasures**
- Team leadership must be visibly present during meetings and decisions.
- The team leader keeps the group focused by preventing the agenda from being hijacked by external events.
- Address team members’ concerns directly, while reinforcing management support and commitment when the team begins to question its purpose.
- In early stages, provide more frequent, explicit direction.

### Reluctant Participants
Members show a lack of participation, give noncommittal feedback, and display disinterest.

**Causes linked to communication**
- A team member has no stake in the outcome and therefore no motivation to speak.
- Intimidation by other team members or leaders.
- Fear of losing a job or position by voicing an opinion, especially when transitioning out of a current role or organization.

**Communication countermeasures**
- Team leaders support active participation and protect members who voice their opinions.
- Leaders must create an environment where it is safe to contribute, ensuring no retaliation occurs.

Both dynamics demonstrate that communication is a two-way obligation. Leaders must broadcast clear direction, while also listening, protecting, and responding to the team’s concerns.

## Communicating the Project Timeline

A raw timeline for the DMAIC phases helps all stakeholders understand the expected pace. The CSSC Training Manual notes that experienced Six Sigma experts assign a number of weeks to each phase and that phases often overlap. A leader either starts with an overall time requirement or works backward from a leadership-imposed deadline. For example, leadership might require an improvement to be completed within four months, leading to a 16-week estimated timeline with overlapping phases (3 weeks for Define, 5 for Measure, and so on).

The most important communication principle here is to frame the timeline as a rough estimate. The manual warns: *"When presenting such a timeline, make sure everyone knows that it is a rough estimate and the time for each phase can change as you go through the process."* Without this clarity, leadership may treat the estimate as a hard deadline, creating unrealistic expectations and damaging the relationship between the team and its sponsor.

## The Critical Path Method as a Communication Tool

The critical path method produces a more detailed timeline and requires more information, so it is typically created during the Define phase once the project team is engaged. The method is itself a communication exercise: the team must jointly identify all critical activities, sequence them, and assign realistic durations. The result is a visual diagram that communicates dependencies, parallel work, and the total phase duration to the team and the sponsor.

The manual provides a step-by-step procedure for building a critical path diagram, using the Define phase of a project to reduce bad debt in medical billing.

**Step 1: Identify critical needs or activities.**  
The team must choose a team, charter the project, define the problem, and create a baseline metric.

**Step 2: Put critical activities in order.**  
The proper sequence is:
- Choose a team.
- Charter the project and define the problem (these can occur simultaneously).
- Create a baseline metric.

**Step 3: Assign a time to each task.**  
A Six Sigma expert estimates:
- Choose a team: 1 week
- Create a charter: 1 week (Monday through Friday)
- Create a problem statement: 1 day (completed on Tuesday)
- Create a baseline metric: 2 weeks

**Step 4: Create the diagram, stacking simultaneous tasks and including time figures.**  
The diagram is drawn left to right. The first block is "Choose a team" with 1 week. The next section stacks "Charter the project (1 week)" and "Create a problem statement (1 day)" because they can be done at the same time. The final block is "Create a baseline metric (2 weeks)."

**Step 5: Draw the critical path through the longest task in each stacked section.**  
When activities are stacked, the critical path takes the longest duration. Although the problem statement is finished on Tuesday, the charter takes the full week. Therefore, the critical path goes through the 1-week charter block.

**Step 6: Add the longest times from each section.**  
Total time = 1 week (choose team) + 1 week (charter, the longer parallel task) + 2 weeks (baseline metric) = 4 weeks for the Define phase.

This systematic, visual approach communicates the logic behind a timeline far better than a single number. It shows the team where parallel work can occur and highlights which tasks must not slip, because any delay on the critical path pushes out the entire phase.

## Milestone Meetings

Once a timeline is established, milestone meetings serve as formal communication points with the project sponsor. In a DMAIC project, milestones are normally set at the end of each phase: Define, Measure, Analyze, Improve, and Control. Sponsors may also require custom milestones, especially if they must approve large funding or resource requests.

The CSSC manual gives an example from a sandwich shop improvement project. The team set these sponsor-facing milestones:

- Define: January 21
- Measure: February 12
- Analyze: February 22
- Improve: March 15
- Control: April 10

These dates create a regular communication cadence. The team or Black Belt meets with the sponsor at each milestone to present findings or results, ensuring continued alignment and visibility.

Teams can also create internal milestones to manage their own tasks. For the Measure phase of the sandwich shop project, the team set these intermediate due dates:

- Definitions of terms: January 25
- Temperature data collected: February 5
- Time observation data collected: February 10

Breaking a phase into smaller, communicated sub-deadlines makes the work more manageable and keeps the entire team on track. Milestone meetings, whether external or internal, transform a timeline from a static document into a living communication tool.

## Budget Communication

Budget management demands clear, sometimes delicate communication. The CSSC manual highlights that not all team members will be aware of financial drivers, and some financial data (such as employee pay) may be restricted. In such cases, the project-leading Black Belt may need to perform certain analyses alone.

Whenever possible, teams benefit when members are made aware of as many financial drivers and constraints as possible. Knowing that a sponsor can only seek a \\$50,000 budget prevents the team from proposing an \\$80,000 capital investment. Informed decisions require this communication.

A Six Sigma leader must have honest, thorough conversations with the sponsor and the organization about:

- How project budgets are calculated (for example, whether only equipment and software costs count, or whether the hours spent by the team are also budgeted).
- How willing the sponsor is to advocate for budget increases.
- The leader’s expected role in maintaining and reporting on the budget.

These discussions ensure that the team works within realistic boundaries and that the sponsor’s expectations are met without surprise.

## Communicating Defined Measures of Success

The final critical communication requirement is a well-defined measure of success. As the CSSC manual warns, if success is not defined at all, the team risks scope creep and a project that never ends. If success is not defined clearly, the team may believe they have succeeded while the sponsor considers the project a failure.

The Define phase must produce a single statement of success that is understood and accepted by all team members, the sponsor, and the customer. That alignment requires deliberate communication: the team cannot assume everyone shares the same vision. Agreement on what “done” looks like is a prerequisite for measuring performance, controlling scope, and declaring the project complete.

## Exam Tips

- When given a scenario describing a team that is indecisive or distracted by organizational changes, select countermeasures that involve leadership presence, frequent direction, and protecting the team from external hijacking, these are communication-focused solutions.
- Expect questions that ask you to interpret a raw DMAIC timeline. The correct answer will emphasize communicating the timeline as a rough estimate to avoid creating a hard deadline.
- You may be presented with a small set of tasks, durations, and parallel possibilities. Apply the critical path method by summing the longest duration from each stacked series; never add the shorter parallel activity.
- Distinguish between sponsor-facing milestones (typically at phase gates) and internal team milestones (task-level due dates). The exam tests your ability to select the appropriate type for a given communication need.
- Recognize that budget communication constraints exist. If sensitive data cannot be shared, the correct answer will direct the Black Belt to handle analysis alone while still making the team aware of high-level financial boundaries to guide realistic decision-making.
